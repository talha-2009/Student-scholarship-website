Rotary Peace Fellowship

## Introduction

Every year, Rotary International brings together a small, carefully selected group of professionals from across the world who share one thing in common: a determination to reduce conflict and build sustainable peace in the communities they come from. The Rotary Peace Fellowship is the vehicle for that ambition. Funded entirely by Rotary and delivered through a network of Rotary Peace Centers at partner universities, it offers mid-career professionals a fully funded route into graduate-level peace and conflict studies, paired with a global community of Rotarians who continue supporting fellows long after they graduate. For anyone already working in humanitarian response, diplomacy, human rights, development, or community mediation, this fellowship is one of the few funded academic programs built specifically around that kind of career.

## About the Opportunity

Unlike open scholarships that fund study at any university a candidate chooses, the Rotary Peace Fellowship channels every fellow into one of a defined set of Rotary Peace Centers, each affiliated with a specific partner university. The objective is not simply to fund a degree, but to build a global network of practitioners who understand both the theory and the practice of peacebuilding, and who can call on each other and on Rotary's worldwide membership throughout their careers. Fellows typically emerge with either a full master's degree or a shorter professional development certificate, depending on the track they are placed into, alongside practical experience gained through an internship or field study built into the program.

## Benefits

Rotary covers the fellowship comprehensively. Full tuition at the assigned Rotary Peace Center is paid directly, removing what is usually the single largest cost of postgraduate study. Travel to and from the host university is also covered, along with room and board for the duration of the program, so fellows are not left covering rent or living costs out of pocket. Perhaps most distinctively, Rotary funds the internship or field-study component that is built into most tracks, which means fellows can gain applied, on-the-ground experience in peacebuilding or conflict resolution without needing to self-fund that placement separately. Beyond the financial support, fellows gain lasting access to the Rotary network itself, a genuinely global body of professionals and civic leaders who often become long-term mentors, collaborators, or employers.

## Who Can Apply?

The fellowship is aimed at professionals, not fresh graduates. Applicants should already have a relevant background in peace, conflict resolution, or international relations, whether through prior study, humanitarian work, diplomatic service, or community-level mediation experience. English proficiency is required, since the program is conducted in English regardless of host country. There is no fixed nationality restriction, and Rotary Peace Fellows are drawn from a genuinely wide range of countries each year. Because the program specifically values applied professional experience alongside academic ability, candidates without any relevant work history are unlikely to be competitive, even with strong grades. If you are uncertain whether your background qualifies, Rotary's own eligibility guidelines on its website are the definitive source, since specific requirements can vary slightly by Peace Center and track.

## Fields of Study or Available Areas

The entire fellowship is built around peace and conflict studies, but that umbrella covers a genuinely broad range of specializations depending on the host Peace Center: conflict resolution and mediation, human rights, humanitarian response, development studies as it relates to post-conflict recovery, and international relations with a security or diplomacy focus. Applicants are generally expected to have some existing connection to one of these areas rather than approaching the fellowship as a first exposure to the field.

## Required Documents

Because applications begin at the local level, the centerpiece of the file is Rotary Club or District endorsement, without which an application cannot proceed. Beyond that, applicants prepare a resume, a personal statement explaining their background and goals, and academic transcripts from previous study. Specific Peace Centers may request additional materials as part of their own admissions process, so applicants should confirm exact requirements once they have identified their preferred track.

## Application Process

The path into this fellowship starts locally, not with Rotary International directly. Prospective applicants need to connect with a Rotary Club or District in their area and express interest in being considered for the Peace Fellowship. That local Club or District reviews the candidate and, if they choose to support the application, formally endorses it before forwarding it to Rotary International for further review. This structure means building a relationship with a local Rotary Club well before the deadline is not optional groundwork, it is the actual first step of the application. Once Rotary International reviews endorsed applications, successful candidates are matched with a specific Rotary Peace Center based on their background and the track they are pursuing.

## Deadline

Application timing for the Rotary Peace Fellowship runs through local Rotary Clubs and Districts, so there is no single global date published for every applicant. Because the local endorsement stage can take real time to arrange, applicants should reach out to a Rotary Club well in advance rather than waiting until close to when they hope to start the program, and should confirm the current cycle's exact deadline directly with Rotary or their local Club.

## Why You Should Apply

Fully funded fellowships built specifically for working professionals in peace and conflict fields are genuinely rare, and the Rotary Peace Fellowship's combination of tuition, living costs, and a funded practical placement makes it a substantive career investment rather than a token award. For professionals already committed to this work, it offers something scholarships for younger students cannot: a cohort of peers who have already spent years in the field, and a global Rotary network that continues opening doors well after the fellowship itself ends. Among international scholarships and fellowships aimed at mid-career professionals, few combine full funding with this level of long-term community. Applicants serious about a career in peacebuilding should start the local Rotary Club conversation early, since that relationship is the true starting point of a strong application.

For full eligibility details, current deadlines, and Peace Center options, applicants should consult the official Rotary Peace Fellowship page directly: https://www.rotary.org/en/our-programs/peace-fellowships
