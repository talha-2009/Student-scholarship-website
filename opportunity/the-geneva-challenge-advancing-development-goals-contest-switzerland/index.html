The Geneva Challenge: Advancing Development Goals Contest

The Geneva Challenge, formally known as the Advancing Development Goals Contest, is an annual international competition run by the Geneva Graduate Institute for teams of Master's students worldwide. Launched in 2014 with the support of Ambassador Jenö Staehelin and once championed by the late Kofi Annan as its High Patron, the contest invites interdisciplinary teams of three to five graduate students to design theoretically grounded, practically implementable solutions to a major global development challenge. The 2026 edition focuses on the future of work, inviting proposals that address how automation, demographic change, and climate transition are reshaping labour markets worldwide, particularly the impact on inequality, youth employment, and the green transition. Teams must combine at least two disciplinary perspectives and can be drawn from a single university or across multiple institutions globally. Submissions are judged first by an interdisciplinary academic steering committee, then by an independent jury of academic, governmental, and private-sector experts. Five finalist teams, one representing each continent category, are invited to Geneva for an oral presentation and public Q&A, with all related travel, visa, and accommodation costs covered. Prizes total CHF 25,000: CHF 10,000 for first place, CHF 5,000 each for two second-place teams, and CHF 2,500 each for two third-place teams. Team registration for the 2026 cycle closes 1 May 2026, with the full proposal submission deadline falling on 11 July 2026 at 23:59 CET, giving registered teams roughly eleven weeks to develop their analysis.

Geneva Challenge Eligibility

The Geneva Challenge is open to current Master's students at any recognised university worldwide. Each team must include three to five members from at least two different academic disciplines, ensuring genuine interdisciplinary collaboration. There are no nationality restrictions, and teams may be drawn from a single institution or span multiple universities across different countries. Doctoral students and recent graduates are not eligible to participate as team members, though they may serve as informal advisors.

Geneva Challenge Benefits and Prizes

Five finalist teams — one from each continent category — are invited to Geneva for an oral presentation and public Q&A session. All travel, visa, and accommodation costs for the finals are fully covered by the organisers. The total prize pool is CHF 25,000: first place receives CHF 10,000, two second-place teams each receive CHF 5,000, and two third-place teams each receive CHF 2,500. Beyond the monetary award, finalists gain visibility among leading development practitioners, policymakers, and academics, and their proposals are published on the Geneva Graduate Institute's platform.

Geneva Challenge Deadline and Timeline

For the 2026 cycle, team registration closes on 1 May 2026. The full proposal submission deadline is 11 July 2026 at 23:59 CET. This gives registered teams approximately eleven weeks to research, draft, and refine their proposals. The finals typically take place in the autumn semester. Always confirm exact dates on the official programme page, as timelines may shift between cycles.

Required Documents for the Geneva Challenge

  • Team registration form — including full names, universities, disciplines, and contact details for all members
  • Proposal document — following the official template, typically 5,000–8,000 words with references
  • Executive summary — a one-page overview of your proposed solution
  • Interdisciplinary justification — explaining how your team combines at least two disciplinary perspectives
  • Presentation slides — required for finalist teams invited to the oral round in Geneva

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. Ignoring the interdisciplinary requirement — teams with members from only one field are disqualified. Ensure your proposal genuinely integrates multiple perspectives.
  2. Submitting a vague proposal — the jury looks for theoretically grounded, practically implementable solutions. Include evidence, methodology, and a clear implementation plan.
  3. Missing the registration deadline — you cannot submit a proposal without first registering your team. Register well before 1 May 2026.
  4. Overlooking the theme — each year has a specific focus. The 2026 theme is the future of work. Proposals that don't address the current theme will not advance.
